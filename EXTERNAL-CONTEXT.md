# Write the Docs — External Context & Evidence Base

Background research supporting the board offsite brief (`BOARD-OFFSITE-BRIEF.md`).
This document gathers external data on where documentation, the profession, and
community/events are heading, so strategy discussion rests on evidence rather
than vibes. Each claim is sourced; figures from secondary sources are flagged.

Prepared: 2026-06-04. Researched from public sources (mid-2026). Treat specific
numbers as directional — they come from varied methodologies.

---

## 1. The AI inflection in documentation

**The profession is being reshaped, not eliminated — but the growth outlook has
cooled.**

- The US Bureau of Labor Statistics' latest projection for technical writers is
  roughly **1% growth over 2024–2034** (about 56,400 jobs, median pay near
  **$91,670**) — a notable markdown from the earlier **7% (2022–2032)**
  projection. BLS explicitly attributes slower growth to **AI raising
  productivity**, not to wholesale job loss. *(Figures via secondary summaries of
  the BLS Occupational Outlook Handbook; verify against bls.gov before citing
  externally.)*
- Industry consensus across practitioner sources: AI **augments** writers,
  shifting value toward curation, fact-checking, information architecture, and
  quality assurance — "AI-assisted knowledge architects" rather than wordsmiths.
- A recurring, under-appreciated finding: AI **speeds up drafting but enlarges
  the maintenance surface** — more content, generated faster, decaying faster.
  That is a structural argument *for* skilled documentarians, not against them.
- **Regulatory tailwind:** the EU AI Act's **Article 4 AI-literacy obligation
  has been in force since 2 February 2025** (enforcement from August 2026),
  requiring organizations to ensure staff AI literacy. Demand for credible,
  vendor-neutral AI-and-docs education is now partly a compliance need.

**Implication for WTD:** This is precisely the anxiety WTD's own community voices
in recent newsletters ("does AI mean people don't need the product?", "how do I
prove documentation's value?"). A trusted, independent community helping
documentarians navigate this is squarely on-mission and timely. **Backs Pillar A.**

## 2. How documentation is consumed now

**AI systems have become a primary consumer of docs — which raises the stakes for
quality, not lowers them.**

- Gartner (via industry reporting) projects **~70% of companies will run
  retrieval-augmented (RAG) bots by 2026**, and **~80% of customer interactions
  will involve chatbots by end of 2025**.
- Source-attributed AI assistants can **deflect 40–50% of routine support
  tickets** — but only when grounded in accurate, current documentation.
- WTD's own community already frames this well: **"AI tools are a new audience
  for documentation,"** LLMs depend on good docs, and people fall back to the
  docs when AI answers fall short.

**Implication for WTD:** The value of good documentation is rising because AI
amplifies both good and bad docs. WTD can own the narrative that **quality,
human-grounded documentation is the foundation the AI era runs on.** **Backs
Pillars A and B.**

## 3. Content authority in the AI era (GEO, not gimmicks)

**Being cited by AI answers is the new visibility — and it rewards exactly the
qualities WTD content already has.**

- "Generative Engine Optimization" (GEO) — getting cited by ChatGPT, Perplexity,
  Google AI Overviews, Claude — is now a recognized discipline. **AI-referred web
  traffic reportedly jumped ~527% year-over-year** in early 2025.
- What actually wins citations: **self-contained passages** that stand without
  context, **answer-first** structure, **fact density** (data/citations
  throughout), authoritative outbound references, and **schema markup**. This
  describes well-structured documentation almost exactly.
- **Reality check on `llms.txt`:** despite a grassroots dev-docs movement, **no
  major AI crawler uses it** as of late 2025, and Google publicly rejected it.
  So the authority play is **not** "publish an `llms.txt` and win" — it is
  **structured, citable, well-linked content plus standard schema markup**, while
  watching for a real "AI crawling protocol" to emerge.

**Implication for WTD:** The Guide, the 94-issue newsletter archive, and the
Topics graph are an unusually strong GEO asset already. Modest structural work
(self-contained answers, schema, clean canonical content) could make WTD a
**default cited source on documentation practice.** **Backs Pillar B; tempers the
`llms.txt` enthusiasm in the improvements report's S2.5.**

## 4. Conference & event landscape

**In-person retains its premium; hybrid is now permanent infrastructure.**

- In-person events still command roughly **60% of event revenue**; virtual ~35%,
  hybrid ~5%. But **~75% of planners now run hybrid formats** and most expect
  hybrid to be a lasting default.
- **~70% of event planners use AI tools** for personalization, scheduling, and
  content — table stakes, not differentiation.

**Implication for WTD:** WTD's existing model — in-person anchor events
(Portland, European) **plus** virtual tickets and global expansion (**Kenya 2025
in Nairobi**, Berlin hybrid) — is well-aligned with where events are heading. The
opportunity is operational leverage (AI-assisted logistics/marketing), not a
format pivot. **Backs Pillars C and D.**

## 5. Community funding & sustainability

**Resilient communities diversify income and convert engagement into support.**

- Sustainable open-source/community orgs use **balanced funding portfolios** —
  commonly a mix like corporate sponsorship, services/offerings, and individual
  giving — and projects with multiple income sources report markedly higher
  sustainability.
- **Tiered membership** is well-received: most contributors prefer tiers with
  varied rewards over flat asks.
- **Engaged communities receive ~60% more financial contributions** than
  disengaged ones — engagement and funding are linked, not separate problems.
- Infrastructure exists to support this without building it in-house: **fiscal
  hosts like Open Source Collective (a 501(c)(6) nonprofit)**, plus GitHub
  Sponsors, Open Collective, and corporate FOSS funds.

**Implication for WTD:** Reliance on a few sponsors plus tickets is a
concentration risk. Options worth weighing: a **supporter/membership tier**,
**diversified sponsorship/grants**, and tying funding asks to the community
engagement WTD already generates. **Backs Pillar C and board Decision #4.**
