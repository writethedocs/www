# Write the Docs — Strategy Brief for the Community Board Offsite

A pre-read to frame strategic discussion and decisions. Deliberately high-level:
it names the choices in front of us, not the implementation. Two companion
documents hold the detail — `IMPROVEMENTS-REPORT.md` (website/product) and
`MARKETING-REPORT.md` (conference/community growth).

Prepared: 2026-06-04 · ~10-minute read · For board discussion, not approval.

---

## 1. Why this matters now

Documentation is in the middle of its biggest shift in a generation. AI is
changing how docs are written, how they're found, and whether organizations
believe they still need writers. Our own community feels it: recent newsletters
are dominated by questions like *"Does AI mean people don't need the product?"*,
*"Is my role safe?"*, and *"How do I prove documentation's value?"*

This is both a threat and the clearest opportunity Write the Docs has had in
years. A global, vendor-neutral community of documentarians is exactly who the
profession needs right now — and our content is, quite literally, part of what
the AI is learning from. The board's job at this offsite is to decide **how
boldly WTD steps into that role**, given that we run on volunteer time.

---

## 2. Where we stand

**Strengths — assets few communities have:**
- A trusted, **vendor-neutral brand** and a 10+ year track record.
- A **content canon**: the Guide; a **94-issue newsletter** distilling Slack
  wisdom into evergreen articles since 2017; a **Topics knowledge graph** linking
  those articles and 10+ years of conference talks; the annual **salary survey**.
- A **global event footprint** (Portland, European, Australia, and now Kenya) plus
  meetups, Slack, and a podcast.
- A values foundation: code of conduct, opportunity grants, inclusive practices.

**Strains — mostly structural:**
- **Everything runs on manual volunteer effort.** Our best processes (the
  newsletter, the Topics graph, conference marketing) are hand-built each cycle.
- **Our assets are under-leveraged.** The talk archive is barely searchable; the
  newsletter archive is buried; the Topics graph lags and is hard to discover.
- **Funnel and funding fragility.** Recurring broken Slack invites, under-told
  sponsor value, and reliance on a small group of organizers.
- **An open identity question** about year-round community vs. pulse events, and
  about how/whether to grow editorial output (the long-running "community blog"
  debate).

The encouraging part: most strains are **leverage problems, not effort problems**
— exactly the kind AI and light automation can relieve, freeing volunteers for
the work only humans can do.

---

## 3. The central strategic question

> **Does WTD choose to be the place the documentation profession turns to as AI
> reshapes the craft — and invest accordingly — or does it continue steady-state
> as a beloved but lightly-resourced community?**

Everything below is a way of making that question concrete and decidable.

---

## 4. Proposed strategic pillars

Four directions to test, refine, or reject at the offsite. They are mutually
reinforcing, but we can't fund all of them at once.

### Pillar A — Lead the profession through the AI transition
Be the trusted, independent convener on what AI means for documentarians:
career, craft, tooling, and proving value. This is the community's most urgent
shared need and squarely on-mission.
*Looks like:* a standing content theme, conference programming, curated guidance,
and a clear WTD point of view.

### Pillar B — Turn our content into lasting infrastructure
Make the Guide + newsletter + talks a durable, discoverable, **citable** canon —
including being the source AI systems quote on documentation. This compounds
brand, mission, and sustainability at once.
*Looks like:* a searchable/transcribed talk archive, an automatically maintained
Topics graph, structured content, and an `llms.txt`-style authority play.

### Pillar C — Make the community sustainable
Reduce volunteer burden through automation, strengthen the funding model, and
build organizer/volunteer succession and meetup-network health.
*Looks like:* automating the manual marketing/editorial chores, a stronger sponsor
value story, and a deliberate volunteer pipeline.

### Pillar D — Grow access and reach
Lean into global expansion (Kenya and beyond), opportunity grants, accessibility,
and adjacent roles entering the field.
*Looks like:* intentional inclusion as a growth strategy, not just a value.

## 5. Decisions for the board

The offsite's real output. Each is a genuine fork, not a foregone conclusion.

1. **Focus.** If we can only do **two** pillars well this year, which two? What do
   we consciously *not* do?
2. **AI posture.** Do we adopt AI internally to relieve volunteer load (editorial
   drafting, social, archive upkeep)? If so, **what is our policy on AI-assisted
   content** — disclosure, human sign-off, quality bar? (We need a stance either
   way; the community will ask.)
3. **Editorial ambition.** Do we expand beyond the monthly newsletter toward a
   broader community publication/blog, and under what curation model? (Resolves a
   multi-year open question.)
4. **Funding.** Is sponsorship-plus-tickets sufficient for the ambition we choose,
   or do we diversify (membership, grants, partnerships)? What would it take to
   fund one part-time coordinator role?
5. **Internationalization.** What is our real stance on translation and
   non-English community — invest, enable volunteers, or decline clearly? (Our most
   persistently requested, repeatedly deferred item.)
6. **Measurement.** Do we commit to a small shared scorecard (signups, attendance,
   CFP submissions, sponsor renewals, newsletter growth) so future decisions are
   evidence-based?

---

## 6. Resourcing reality

- WTD's binding constraint is **volunteer attention**, not ideas or goodwill.
- The highest-leverage plays are ones where **AI/automation does the production
  work and humans keep the judgment** — drafting, tagging, repurposing, archive
  upkeep. These *create* volunteer capacity.
- Anything requiring sustained new manual effort (a hand-curated blog, ongoing
  translation, more events) needs an honest owner and funding before we commit.
- A modest investment in coordination (even part-time) may unlock
  disproportionate volunteer output.

---

## 7. Risks to weigh

- **Relevance risk:** if WTD is silent on AI, the conversation moves elsewhere.
- **Burnout risk:** over-committing volunteers is the most likely failure mode.
- **Brand/quality risk:** moving fast on AI-generated content without a clear
  policy could erode the trust that is our core asset.
- **Funding concentration:** dependence on a few sponsors and a few organizers.
- **Doing-too-much risk:** spreading thin across all four pillars achieves none.

---

## 8. Suggested offsite flow

1. **Align on the moment** (§1–§3) — do we accept the central question? *(short)*
2. **Pressure-test the pillars** (§4) — keep, cut, reframe. *(core session)*
3. **Make the six decisions** (§5) — force-rank focus; assign owners. *(core)*
4. **Resourcing & funding** (§6) — match ambition to capacity honestly.
5. **Define success** (§5.6) — agree the scorecard and a 12-month checkpoint.
6. **Commit** — 2–3 priorities, named owners, next review date.

**Discussion prompts:**
- What would make WTD *indispensable* to a documentarian in 2027?
- If we did nothing new this year, what would we most regret in three?
- Where are we spending volunteer effort that automation could absorb?
- What's the one thing only WTD can do for this profession right now?
